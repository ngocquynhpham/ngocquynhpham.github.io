5 Secrets to a Thriving Monstera Deliciosa

Master the art of caring for the Swiss Cheese Plant with our expert guide.

Sarah Green

Botanist ‚Ä¢ Oct 12, 2023 ‚Ä¢ 5 min read

The Monstera Deliciosa, often called the Swiss Cheese Plant, has become a staple in modern interior design. Its iconic split leaves and tropical vibe can transform any dull corner into a vibrant green sanctuary. But keeping this jungle giant happy indoors requires a bit more than just admiration.

Whether you're a seasoned plant parent or a beginner looking to adopt your first green friend, these five secrets will ensure your Monstera doesn't just survive, but thrives.

1. Finding the Perfect Light Balance

Many assume that tropical plants need direct sunlight, but in the jungle, Monsteras grow under the canopy of larger trees. This means they are accustomed to bright, filtered light. Direct afternoon sun can scorch their leaves, leaving unsightly brown marks.

"Think of the lighting as if you were wearing sunglasses. Bright, but not blinding."

Place your Monstera near an east-facing window where it can catch the gentle morning sun, or a few feet away from a south-facing window. If your room is dark, consider a grow light, but don't expect rapid growth in low-light conditions.

2. The "Soak and Dry" Watering Method

Overwatering is the number one killer of indoor plants. Monstera roots are susceptible to rot if they sit in soggy soil. The best approach is the "soak and dry" method. Wait until the top 2-3 inches of soil are completely dry before watering again.

Checking soil moisture is key to preventing root rot.

3. Humidity is Your Friend

Coming from the rainforests of Central America, these plants crave humidity. Typical homes can be dry, especially in winter. Increasing humidity helps the leaves unfold without tearing and keeps them glossy. You can mist your plant regularly, place a pebble tray with water beneath the pot, or invest in a humidifier.

4. Support its Climbing Nature

In the wild, Monsteras climb up trees. To encourage larger leaves and dramatic fenestrations (the holes in the leaves), provide a moss pole for the aerial roots to cling to. As the plant grows taller, gently tie the stem to the pole. This mimics its natural habitat and signals the plant to produce mature foliage.

5. Cleaning and Pruning

Dust can block the stomata (pores) on the leaves, hindering photosynthesis. Wipe the leaves down with a damp cloth once a month. This is also a great time to inspect for pests like spider mites or scale. Don't be afraid to prune off yellowing or damaged leaves at the base of the stem; this directs energy to new growth.
